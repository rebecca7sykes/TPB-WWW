Glossary of Terms
Understatement
A technique of creating emphasis by saying less than is actually or literally
true. Understatement is the opposite of hyperbole or exaggeration, and can be used to create humor
as well as biting satire. See
Hyperbole
Verb
A word, or set of words, that expresses action or state of being.
Verbal
A word that is derived from a verb and has the power of a verb, but acts like another
part of speech. Like a verb, a verbal may take an object, a modifier, and sometimes a subject; but
unlike a verb, a verbal functions like a noun, an adjective, or an adverb. Three types of verbals are
gerunds, infinitives, and participles. Also, pertaining to words, either written or spoken. See
Oral
Verse
A unit of poetry such as a stanza or line. See
Poetry, Stanza
Voice
Indicates whether the subject is acting or being acted upon. Active voice indicates that the
subject is acting¡ª doing something. (Benjamin Franklin discovered the secrets of electricity.)
Passive voice indicates that the subject is being acted upon (The secrets of electricity were discovered
by Benjamin Franklin). Also, a writer 's unique use of language that allows a reader to perceive a
human personality in his or her writing. The elements of style that determine a writer's voice
include sentence structure, diction, and tone. The term can also be applied to the narrator of a
selection. See
Diction, Tone
