1. Visual-gestural skills provide a means of
communication with Deaf people in the context of the
Deaf culture.
Students can:
? use receptive skills to comprehend simple statements
and questions in standard dialect
? express basic needs and compose statements, questions
and short messages to signers of the same age group and
to familiar adults
? initiate and engage in simple face-to-face conversations.
This is evident, for example, when students:
v
carry on informal, interactive, face-to-face conversations with
familiar individuals who are deaf
v
understand age-appropriate narratives, including stories,
folktales, and other expressions of the Deaf culture
v
use repetition and rephrasing to sustain comprehension with
more skilled signers
v
use gestures to compose simple statements and questions
v
use appropriate vocabulary and structure when communicating
with native ASL signers
v
make their messages comprehensible to fluent signers through
repetition and rephrasing.

NOTE: American Sign Language (ASL) is a visual-gestural language, devoid
of voice, and it does not have a written form. It is governed by sign
production, registers, and nonmanual grammar using facial and body signals.
The emphasis on communication is applied in situations characterized as
receptive, expressive, and interactive.
