1. Communication in Native languages takes place
primarily to share expression of ideas, thoughts, and
feelings to preserve Native cultural ideology.
Students can:
? comprehend messages and short conversations when
listening to Native speakers; repetition may be necessary
for full understanding
? initiate and sustain conversations with some hesitation
and difficulty in pronunciation in short and familiar
communicative situations
? produce an extended communication through a series of
short connected utterances, using repetition as needed
? comprehend the meaning of myths and legends told to
them repeatedly by Native speakers.
This is evident, for example, when students:
v
relate an incident, using common verbal structures accurately,
although errors occur in more complex patterns
v
engage in a simulated classroom dialogue
v
respond in complete sentences to questions from Native
speakers
v
make an oral presentation about a traditional festival
v
compare similar myths from differing groups of Native people.
2. Writing systems, and therefore reading, are recent
introductions to Native languages and are used
primarily to encourage functional communication in
the listening and speaking skills.
Students can:
? understand simple narrative and descriptive authentic
materials and edited texts within a familiar context
? read Native texts or stories aloud with accurate
pronunciation and attention to dialogue
? write short communications that are comprehensible to
Native speakers used to dealing with students. Difficulties
still occur when expressing more complex thoughts.
This is evident, for example, when students:
v
read and comprehend passages with familiar sentence patterns
v
acquire essential points of information from simple narrative
and descriptive writing
v
guess meaning of writing on topics of special interest by using
context clues
v
write simple notes, letters, and short reports
v
use elementary vocabulary and commonly encountered
structures accurately
v
express themselves in present, future, and past time frames.
Students will be able to use a language other than English for communication.
Native American Languages
9
NOTE: The primary purpose of instruction in Native languages is to teach children
how to speak Native languages and to ensure that these languages do not become
extinct. These languages have been historically in the oral tradition, and there is no
intent to move away from that tradition. It is only
in the past 20 years, with the introduction of Native American language programs in
the schools, that a writing system has been developed. Each Nation will have to
decide to what extent a writing system will be used in instruction.
