1. Visual-gestural skills provide a means of
communication with Deaf people in the context of the
Deaf culture.
Students can:
? understand a wide range of registers delivered with
some repetition and paraphrasing by fluent ASL signers,
comprehension may be hindered when the topics are
unfamiliar or when more advanced signed
communication is being used
? organize presentations on everyday topics
? express complex ideas with confidence.
This is evident, for example, when students:
v
determine the essential content of face-to-face discussions or
presentations, of signed feature films, and of signed programs
on television and videotape
v
understand communications on a wide range of topics, and on
issues of concern globally and in Deaf culture
v
recognize learned vocabulary and structures as well as those
acquired through independent exposure to the language
v
demonstrate good control of the morphology of the language and
of the most frequently used syntactic structures, although errors
may still occur
v
use culturally appropriate behaviors regularly.
2. Interactive communication is possible with Deaf
people through the use of signs.
Students can:
? converse with confidence
? engage in extended discourse with native ASL signers on
a broad range of topics including those of general
interest to the Deaf culture.
This is evident, for example, when students:
v
use appropriate learned vocabulary and structures
v
employ simple and complex sentences and all conversational
tenses to communicate in all time frames
v
use culturally appropriate behaviors of the Deaf community.

NOTE: American Sign Language (ASL) is a visual-gestural language, devoid
of voice, and it does not have a written form. It is governed by sign
production, registers, and nonmanual grammar using facial and body signals.
The emphasis on communication is applied in situations characterized as
receptive, expressive, and interactive.
