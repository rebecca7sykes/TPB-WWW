Communication
Communication is at the heart of foreign language study. The ability
to communicate is increasingly important in a global world.
For American students, the ability to function directly in at
least one language other than English will become
increasingly important in the rapidly shrinking,
interdependent world of the twenty-first century... They must
be able to participate appropriately in face-to-face
interaction with members of other societies, and they must
also be able to interpret the concepts, ideas, and opinions
expressed by members of these societies through their media
and their literatures.
(SFFLL, p. 35).
To be communicatively competent in a foreign language, a student
must convey and receive messages successfully by combining the
knowledge of the language system with the knowledge of the cultural
conventions. For this reason, culture and language are closely
intertwined.
The
Standards for Foreign Language Learning
characterize
communication in three communicative modes that place primary
emphasis on the context and purpose of the communication (Brecht
& Walton, 1994). The three modes are:
1. The Interpersonal Mode
2. The Interpretive Mode
3. The Presentational Mode
